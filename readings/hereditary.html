Hereditary

Conflict of Interest, Autoimmune Grief, and the God Who Gives Answers
Ari Aster, 2018 · A critical reading · April 2026

See also: The Audience's Séance — what real Reddit threads, Letterboxd, and fan discourse reveal about whether Aster is trolling us

The Reading

Hereditary is a film about a family that destroys itself through the same mechanisms it developed to survive. The protective responses of each family member begin reading the other members' protective responses as threats. The system becomes adversarial — not because anyone chose malice, but because the defending is the damage.

This is autoimmune disorder as family dynamic. The immune response learns from the immune response. The pathogen drops out. The system overfits to its own noise. Each member's defensive behavior becomes the training data for the next member's defensive behavior, recursively, until the original cause is irrelevant.

Annie builds miniatures — scale models of her life that she can see all at once, hold at arm's length. She seeks not knowledge but relief. Her knowledge-seeking is instrumental: she doesn't want to understand, she wants it to stop. This is what makes her vulnerable. Relief-seeking lowers your evidentiary standard. You accept the theory that promises the pain stops.

The film's single deepest idea can be stated simply: conflict of interest. She can't be the artist and the subject. She can't be the investigator and the suspect. She can't be the judge and the accused. You can't properly investigate yourself. A family cannot properly investigate itself. When you turn the investigative apparatus inward, the investigation becomes the new damage.

The Miscalibration of Annie Graham

Toni Collette plays Annie as someone whose emotional register is always slightly miscalibrated relative to the room. Her interior experience is so consuming that she cannot model how she appears from the outside. This is not madness — it is a precise characterization of someone who has lived so long inside her own processing that the gap between interior and exterior has become invisible to her.

Steve Jesus Christ, Annie. You're not planning on letting him see that, are you?

Annie Who?

Steve Peter. How do you think he's gonna feel when he sees that?

Annie What? It's not about him.

Annie No. It's a neutral view of the accident.

"It's not about him" is not cruelty. It is the statement of someone for whom Peter genuinely did not enter the equation. Steve's concern is about a viewer. Annie doesn't register that a viewer exists. The miniature has no audience. The gallery is gone. Steve isn't the audience. The word "neutral" is doing one thing in the dialogue: setting up the dinner scene sixty seconds later, where any claim to neutrality is demolished.

At dinner, Peter's counter-accusation lands: "She didn't want to go to the party. So why was she there?" Annie is not neutral. She never was. And this is exactly the vulnerability Joan will later exploit — "You didn't kill her, Annie" — planting a seed which is a craving that can be sated only by knowledge: the confirmation that her daughter would not point the finger at her.

Annie Don't you swear at me, you little shit! Don't you ever raise your voice at me! I am your mother! Do you understand? All I do is worry and slave and defend you. And all I get back is that fucking face on your face. So full of disdain and resentment and always so annoyed. Well, now your sister is dead.

...

Peter What about you, Mom? She didn't wanna go to the party. So why was she there?

The Miniatures: From Professional to Compulsive

The miniatures undergo a three-stage transformation across the film, tracked through dialogue and screenplay directions:

Stage 1 — Professional art (distanced, for strangers)

Steve Hey, how's it going?

Annie Mmm, just working. Taking a break from avoiding the show.

Steve So, you still toiling away at the hospice?

Annie Yeah, between this and the, uh, preschool.

Steve So what's our deadline now? Seven months?

Annie Six and a half.

The hospice and preschool miniatures — the grandmother, Charlie — are gallery-bound. There is a deadline, a show title pending. The tone is warm, domestic, professional. The subjects of the miniatures are alive.

Stage 2 — Destruction

Both subjects die. The gallery calls offering to postpone the show. Annie, frustrated, smashes her professional work. The sanctioned frame is broken.

Gallery voicemail from Patrick at the Archer Gallery plays. Annie gasps, screams — smashing sounds.

Steve Oh, jeez. That smell.

Annie I didn't feel like looking at it anymore.

Note: the smell is not the smashed miniatures. It is the grandmother's corpse, which the cult has placed in the attic — a mystery planted here and resolved much later. Aster associates the two events at the same moment in the film: professional collapse and supernatural intrusion, simultaneous.

Stage 3 — Private reconstruction, then prosecution

After destroying the gallery work, Annie builds the accident recreation. Not for the gallery. Not for anyone. She calls it "neutral" — and sixty seconds later reveals at dinner that she has already reached a verdict. The same practice serves three completely different psychological functions. Annie can't see the transitions because the activity never changes. She is still just building little rooms.

Steve's line — one of the most underrated in the film — identifies what she is doing as devotional:

Steve You can always build a shrine to all the terrible things in the world, but it doesn't mean that you have to destroy anything that is good.

The Classroom: Aster's Thesis Statement

Peter is in the classroom when the teacher discusses Sophocles. The question is posed directly to the character who will become the answer:

Teacher So he thinks he has control. But let's all remember, Sophocles wrote the oracle so that it was unconditional, meaning, Heracles never had any choice, right? So does that make it more tragic or less tragic that if he has a choice?

Girl I think it's more tragic because if it's all just inevitable, then that means that the characters had no hope. They never had hope because they're all just, like, hopeless. They're all like pawns in this horrible, hopeless machine.

The film's answer: both simultaneously. Annie has a fatal flaw — the consuming interiority, the need to build models and theories and solve — and the outcome was also fixed. Her flaw is the mechanism by which the predetermined outcome executes. She was always going to try to solve it, and trying to solve it was always going to be the thing that completed the ritual.

Paimon — "King Paimon (God of Mischief)" in the screenplay — is described in the demonological source text as a king who bestows knowledge, teaches arts and sciences, reveals hidden things, answers questions. He is an answerer. The demon is defined in relief by the craving. Paimon is the shape of the hole that need creates.

Interpretive Framework

Conflict of Interest
The unifying principle. Annie cannot be both the investigator and the suspect, the artist and the subject, the judge and the accused. A family cannot properly investigate itself. Turning the apparatus inward is the damage.
Autoimmune Disorder
The body's defense system misidentifies part of itself as a threat. The mechanism designed to protect becomes the mechanism of destruction — not because it's broken, but because it's functioning correctly against the wrong target.
Overfitting
The mechanism by which immune response becomes autoimmune disorder. The system trains on its own noise. Annie takes corrupted data (the séance, Joan's guidance), builds a model with total confidence, and acts on it. The data was adversarial.
Relief, Not Curiosity
Annie is not an inquisitive person. She is repulsed by the séance; Joan strong-arms her. She seeks relief, not knowledge. The knowledge-seeking is instrumental — she doesn't want to understand, she wants it to stop. This is what makes her vulnerable.
The Doll-Maker's Position
Annie builds the stage, arranges the little people. A real god comes along and uses her stage to arrange the real people. She's not a god. She's a god's infrastructure — the useful idiot who builds the apparatus of control and then gets controlled through it.
Paimon as Named Absence
The demon is what families do to themselves when they turn the investigative apparatus inward under a conflict of interest. The cult hasn't summoned something — they've named something. The cult is people who have given a name to the shape of the hole.

Is Aster Trolling Us?

The cult's story is the most satisfying narrative because it accounts for everything. Every tragedy has a cause. Every coincidence was orchestrated. Every death was a step in a ritual. The sigil on the telephone pole. The grandmother's body in the attic. Joan's long con. It all connects.

But the naked cult members bowing in a treehouse. The demon coronation. Joan's speech. "Hail, Paimon!" It is ridiculous. It reads like a completely different film from the dinner scene. And maybe that tonal break — the hokeyness — is the hallmark of trolling.

The film has three nested layers of the same transaction: the cult wants all the answers from Paimon, Annie wants all the answers from Joan, and we want all the answers from Aster. And Aster is serving us the sigil on the telephone pole in the same way Joan serves Annie the séance — here, look, it was all planned, it all makes sense, consume this explanation and your distress will resolve.

He could be making fun of us as we swallow it. A cautionary tale about people who read too much into things — built by inventing a cult of people who want all the answers, who create mayhem for a woman who tries to avoid her distress by finding answers.

But he doesn't commit to that reading either. He doesn't undercut the cult. The supernatural is real in the film. Things levitate. People float. He won't let us dismiss it. He makes us sit in exactly the position the classroom scene describes: is it more tragic if the gods did it, or if nobody did?

He is not answering. He leaves us wondering. And that not-answering may be the film's final hereditary trait — passed down from Aster to us.

Open Questions

The miniatures. We have not fully solved what they are for Annie. "Neutral" is wrong. "Control" is too easy. "Shrine" (Steve's word) may be closest. The professional/private distinction matters because it maps the collapse of the boundary between investigating the world and investigating yourself. But the precise function remains unsettled.

Charlie's interiority. If Charlie was always Paimon, was there ever a Charlie? The film wants it both ways — genuine childlike confusion and demonic inhabitation. "I want grandma" could be either. The possession is not identity. They coexist but are not merged. But where Charlie ends and Paimon begins is deliberately left unresolved.

Wealth. "The riches to the conjurer." "Our sacrifice will pale next to the rewards." The Paimon lore promises wealth. Nobody in the film seems motivated by money. Either Aster imported demonological lore without fully digesting it, or the wealth detail is doing something we cannot yet see.

Midsommar. Aster's next film. Does it answer any of this, or does it repeat the structure with different furniture?
