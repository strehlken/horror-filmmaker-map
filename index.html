A handful of critics have followed this wave closely enough to have a coherent map of the whole landscape. Their perspectives help locate which films speak to which sensibility — and why the wave happened when it did.

David Ehrlich
IndieWire — Chief Film Critic

Ehrlich has been the most consistent journalistic champion of this wave, treating it not as a genre trend but as the most vital current in contemporary cinema. His frame: horror is uniquely positioned to name collective anxieties that other genres soften. He argued early that Hereditary deserved the same critical vocabulary applied to Bergman. Gave Barbarian a rare A, called Midsommar "triumphant even in its self-indulgence." He tends to rate the more formally ambitious work highly even when it frustrates — Beau is Afraid divided most critics; Ehrlich was among the few who defended its logic. His ongoing argument is that horror's low-culture reputation has caused critics to miss the sharpest filmmaking of the decade.

Bilge Ebiri
Vulture / New York Times

Ebiri has written specifically about the horror renaissance as a cultural moment, not just a series of good films. His frame sits at the intersection of film history and sociology: the best horror always reflects the moment it was made in, and this wave is inseparable from the post-2016 atmosphere of paranoia, bodily anxiety, and institutional failure. He's particularly strong on the Peele films' relationship to the long tradition of Black American Gothic — and on what Fargeat and the body horror strand are doing that the male-authored genre never quite managed. Thoughtful on Sinners as a film about what it means to make art while being systematically erased.

Brian Tallerico
RogerEbert.com — Managing Editor

The most consistent across the entire landscape — has reviewed nearly everything here and brings a frame that's less theoretical than Ehrlich or Ebiri, more interested in craft and genre mechanics. His Weapons review compared Cregger to Paul Thomas Anderson (Magnolia) and George Romero (The Crazies) in the same breath, which tells you how he holds the genre. His criterion: ambition has to be earned by execution. He defends Beau is Afraid less than Ehrlich. He was early on Barbarian and gave it four stars. His overall take on the wave: horror has earned its prestige, but the films that deserve it are the ones that deliver genre satisfaction alongside the ideas — not one at the expense of the other.

What you're looking at

This is not a map of all horror. It's a specific slice — roughly 2014 to 2025 — sometimes called elevated horror or prestige horror, though most of the filmmakers on it resist those labels. The wave emerged when a set of directors started treating horror as a formal language for ideas rather than a delivery mechanism for scares.

The filter that produced this map

Three conditions, roughly: (1) Authorial voice — you could identify these directors in a single frame. (2) Critical traction beyond genre fandom — reviews in mainstream outlets, awards consideration, serious essay coverage. (3) Thematic ambition — the horror is in service of something: grief, race, bodily autonomy, community collapse, systemic violence.

What is not here

Franchise horror (The Conjuring universe, Insidious, Annabelle). Blumhouse's commercial output. Slasher revivals. Found footage. James Wan, who is skilled, but operating in a different register. Mike Flanagan (The Haunting of Hill House, Midnight Mass, The Fall of the House of Usher) is the significant omission — his work is excellent and would sit in the folk/psychological cluster, but it's primarily streaming television, which feels like a separate conversation about a different medium.

Where it started

The Babadook (2014) is the origin point most critics agree on — the film that proved horror could be entirely, unambiguously about what it appeared to be about (grief, single motherhood, depression) without sacrificing genre effectiveness. It arrived before A24 had consolidated its identity and gave critics a new template.

Get Out (2017) was the tipping point. It made the wave impossible to ignore, won the Oscar for original screenplay, and proved the commercial argument. After Get Out, studios understood this could work at scale.

The A24 effect

A24 created an aesthetic permission structure. Eggers, Aster, and Ti West all made their defining films through A24. The studio proved that art-cinema sensibility and horror genre mechanics could share a marketplace — and that the audience existed. The A24 aesthetic (slow, cold, image-obsessed, thematically loaded) became so influential that films not released by A24 started being described in those terms anyway.

What connects it all

These films share a conviction that horror's job is not just to scare but to name something true about right now. They also share patience — a willingness to let dread build rather than deliver scares on schedule. And they share a high tolerance for ambiguity: most of them refuse to resolve cleanly, which is itself a formal argument about the world they're depicting.
